Systematic Troubleshooting Approach

When issues arise in stick welding, a systematic approach helps identify and resolve problems quickly and effectively.

Five-Step Troubleshooting Process

  1. Identify the Problem: Accurately recognize the specific issue
  2. Determine Possible Causes: List potential factors contributing to the problem
  3. Test Solutions: Make one change at a time, testing after each adjustment
  4. Implement the Fix: Apply the solution that resolves the issue
  5. Prevent Recurrence: Document what worked and update procedures

Key Variables to Check

When troubleshooting, consider these variables in the following order:

  1. Equipment Function: Is the machine working correctly?
  2. Material Preparation: Is the material clean and properly prepared?
  3. Electrode Selection: Is the electrode appropriate for the application?
  4. Electrode Condition: Is the electrode dry and undamaged?
  5. Welding Parameters: Are amperage, polarity, and arc length correct?
  6. Technique: Is the welding technique suitable for the application?
  7. Environmental Factors: Are wind, moisture, or temperature affecting the weld?

Keep a welding log of problems encountered and solutions that worked. This creates a personalized troubleshooting guide that becomes more valuable over time.

Understanding Cause and Effect

Welding defects are rarely random. Most issues have specific causes that follow consistent patterns:

  • Consistent Defects: If the same problem occurs repeatedly, look for systematic issues (machine settings, material preparation, technique)
  • Intermittent Defects: If problems come and go, look for variable factors (drafts, electrode sticking, inconsistent technique)
  • Progressive Defects: If problems worsen over time, look for cumulative factors (heat buildup, electrode moisture absorption, equipment degradation)

Arc Starting and Stability Issues

Difficulty Starting the Arc

Symptoms:

  • Electrode sticks to workpiece
  • Arc initiates but immediately goes out
  • Inconsistent starts
  • Multiple attempts needed to start arc

Causes:

  1. Low Open Circuit Voltage: Insufficient voltage to initiate arc
  2. Damp Electrodes: Moisture interferes with arc initiation
  3. Poor Connections: High resistance in circuit
  4. Improper Technique: Incorrect striking method
  5. Wrong Polarity: Incorrect polarity for electrode type
  6. Surface Contamination: Oil, paint, or rust present

Solutions:

  1. Check Machine Settings:
    • Verify open circuit voltage is sufficient (minimum 50V)
    • Ensure amperage is appropriate for electrode size
    • Confirm polarity matches electrode requirements
  2. Check Electrode Condition:
    • Use fresh, dry electrodes
    • Verify electrodes are stored properly
    • Try different electrode type if issues persist
  3. Inspect Connections:
    • Ensure tight, clean connections at electrode holder
    • Check work clamp for solid connection
    • Inspect cables for damage
  4. Improve Technique:
    • For scratch start: Use light, quick motion like striking a match
    • For tap start: Tap lightly and immediately raise to establish arc
    • Start on clean, bare metal area

Unstable Arc

Symptoms:

  • Arc sputters or pops
  • Inconsistent arc sound
  • Arc wanders or deflects
  • Arc extinguishes unexpectedly

Causes:

  1. Arc Length Issues: Too long or inconsistent arc length
  2. Incorrect Amperage: Too high or too low
  3. Electrode Problems: Moisture, coating damage
  4. Arc Blow: Magnetic forces deflecting arc
  5. Power Supply Fluctuations: Inconsistent power
  6. Surface Contaminants: Oil, rust, paint

Solutions:

  1. Control Arc Length:
    • Maintain consistent arc length (approx. = electrode diameter)
    • Use arm rest or support for stability
    • Practice steady hand movements
  2. Adjust Amperage:
    • Increase amperage if arc is weak or stubborn
    • Decrease amperage if arc is harsh or erratic
    • Stay within the electrode's recommended range
  3. Address Arc Blow:
    • Reposition work clamp
    • Switch to AC if using DC
    • Angle electrode slightly into the blow direction
    • Wrap ground cable around workpiece
  4. Check Power Supply:
    • Ensure generator has sufficient capacity
    • Check for shared circuits causing fluctuations
    • Inspect for loose connections

Arc Blow

Symptoms:

  • Arc deflects to one side
  • Arc wanders unpredictably
  • Difficult to control arc direction
  • Deflection typically worse at starts/stops and corners

Causes:

  1. Magnetic Fields: DC current creates magnetic field around arc
  2. Work Clamp Position: Current path creates unbalanced field
  3. Joint Configuration: Corner joints and ends of welds
  4. Base Metal: Some steels have magnetic properties
  5. Nearby Ferromagnetic Objects: Tools, fixtures

Solutions:

  1. Work Clamp Positioning:
    • Position clamp in direction of intended weld
    • Use multiple work clamps for large assemblies
    • Move clamp as weld progresses for long welds
  2. Current Type:
    • Switch to AC current (eliminates magnetic field issues)
    • If DC is required, use shorter arc length
  3. Welding Technique:
    • Use backstep or skip welding technique
    • Angle electrode slightly against blow direction
    • Increase travel speed
    • Use shorter arc length
  4. Physical Solutions:
    • Wrap ground cable around workpiece (creates counter-magnetic field)
    • Place steel bars parallel to weld to provide alternate paths
    • Pre-place tack welds to distribute magnetic fields
    • Remove nearby ferromagnetic objects

Weld Quality Defects

Porosity

Symptoms:

  • Visible holes or pores in weld bead
  • Surface porosity (visible holes)
  • Subsurface porosity (only visible after testing)
  • Clustered or scattered tiny holes

Causes:

  1. Contaminated Base Metal: Oil, grease, rust, paint, moisture
  2. Damp Electrodes: Moisture in electrode coating
  3. Improper Arc Length: Arc too long
  4. Improper Travel Speed: Too fast for gas to escape
  5. Wind or Drafts: Disrupting shielding gas
  6. Improper Electrode: Wrong electrode for material

Solutions:

  1. Proper Material Preparation:
    • Clean base metal thoroughly (grind, wire brush, solvent)
    • Remove all paint, rust, oil, grease, moisture
    • Pre-heat if needed to drive off surface moisture
  2. Electrode Management:
    • Store electrodes in dry conditions
    • Use rod ovens for low hydrogen electrodes
    • Discard electrodes that have been exposed to moisture
  3. Technique Adjustments:
    • Maintain shorter arc length
    • Slow travel speed to allow gases to escape
    • Use proper manipulation technique for electrode type
  4. Environmental Control:
    • Use windscreens in outdoor conditions
    • Eliminate drafts from fans or ventilation
    • Position work to minimize environmental effects

Incomplete Fusion

Symptoms:

  • Lack of bonding between weld metal and base metal
  • Visible areas where weld doesn't blend with base metal
  • Gap or separation between weld and base metal
  • Cold lap or overlap at edges

Causes:

  1. Insufficient Heat Input: Amperage too low
  2. Travel Speed Too Fast: Inadequate time for fusion
  3. Improper Electrode Angle: Not directing heat properly
  4. Surface Contamination: Preventing proper wetting
  5. Improper Joint Preparation: Poor fit-up or design
  6. Wrong Electrode Selection: Not suited for the application

Solutions:

  1. Adjust Welding Parameters:
    • Increase amperage to provide adequate heat
    • Ensure correct polarity for electrode type
    • Use electrode diameter appropriate for joint thickness
  2. Improve Technique:
    • Slow travel speed to allow better fusion
    • Maintain proper work angle and travel angle
    • Ensure arc is directed at leading edge of weld pool
    • Use slight weaving motion if needed to ensure sidewall fusion
  3. Joint Preparation:
    • Clean joint faces thoroughly
    • Create proper bevel angles for thick materials
    • Ensure appropriate root gap
    • Remove all contaminants (oil, rust, paint, etc.)
  4. Electrode Selection:
    • Choose electrode with appropriate penetration characteristics
    • Use E6010/E6011 for better penetration in critical applications
    • Ensure electrode is appropriate for base material

Undercut

Symptoms:

  • Groove melted into base metal at weld toe
  • Insufficient filling at edge of weld
  • Linear depression along weld edge
  • Reduced thickness in parent material at weld edge

Causes:

  1. Excessive Amperage: Too much heat melting base metal
  2. Improper Electrode Angle: Not directing filler properly
  3. Arc Length Too Long: Poor control of molten pool
  4. Travel Speed Too Fast: Insufficient time for filling
  5. Excessive Manipulation: Too wide a weave pattern
  6. Poor Technique: No pause at weld toes during weaving

Solutions:

  1. Adjust Welding Parameters:
    • Reduce amperage to appropriate level
    • Maintain shorter arc length
    • Use electrode diameter appropriate for joint
  2. Improve Electrode Control:
    • Hold correct work angle (typically 10-20° from perpendicular)
    • Focus arc on leading edge of weld pool, not on side walls
    • Maintain consistent electrode extension
  3. Adjust Travel Speed:
    • Slow down to allow proper filling at toes
    • Use consistent travel speed
    • When using weave pattern, slow at edges
  4. Weaving Technique:
    • Pause momentarily at each side of the weave
    • Reduce weave width (not more than 2-3 times electrode diameter)
    • Use J or C weave pattern to control heat at edges

Excessive Reinforcement

Symptoms:

  • Weld metal stacked too high above base material
  • Excessively convex bead profile
  • Weld height disproportionate to width
  • Creates stress concentration at weld toe

Causes:

  1. Travel Speed Too Slow: Excessive deposit in one area
  2. Current Too Low: Poor weld metal flow and wetting
  3. Improper Electrode Angle: Depositing rather than flowing metal
  4. Improper Electrode Size: Too large for application
  5. Poor Joint Design: Insufficient space for proper profile

Solutions:

  1. Adjust Travel Speed:
    • Increase travel speed for flatter bead profile
    • Maintain consistent speed
  2. Modify Welding Parameters:
    • Increase amperage within electrode's range
    • Use larger diameter electrode with higher amperage
  3. Adjust Technique:
    • Use slight weaving motion to spread weld pool
    • Hold proper work angle to promote wetting
    • Control arc length for better flow
  4. Modify Joint Design:
    • Widen root opening if possible
    • Adjust bevel angle for better access
    • Consider adapting to multi-pass procedure

Structural Defects

Cracking

Symptoms:

  • Visible linear fractures in or adjacent to weld
  • Hot cracks: Occur during or immediately after welding
  • Cold cracks: Develop hours or days after welding
  • Longitudinal, transverse, crater, or heat-affected zone cracks

Common Causes by Crack Type:

Hot Cracking:
  • High sulfur or phosphorus content in base metal
  • High restraint during solidification
  • Poor joint design creating high stress concentration
  • Improper electrode selection
Cold Cracking (Hydrogen-Induced):
  • Hydrogen in weld metal (damp electrodes)
  • High hardness in heat-affected zone
  • High restraint/residual stress
  • Inadequate preheat or interpass temperature
Crater Cracking:
  • Improper termination technique
  • Ending weld abruptly
  • Insufficient crater fill

Solutions:

  1. Prevent Hydrogen Cracking:
    • Use low hydrogen electrodes (E7018)
    • Store electrodes properly (rod ovens)
    • Clean joint surfaces thoroughly
    • Apply appropriate preheat:
      • Carbon steel < 1/2": Generally no preheat required
      • Carbon steel 1/2" - 1": 200-300°F
      • Carbon steel > 1": 300-500°F
      • High-carbon or alloy steel: 400-700°F
    • Maintain interpass temperature
    • Slow cooling rate after welding
  2. Prevent Hot Cracking:
    • Use appropriate filler metal for base material
    • Reduce joint restraint when possible
    • Use proper joint design to minimize stress
    • Control heat input (not too high)
    • Apply proper weld sequence to distribute stress
  3. Prevent Crater Cracks:
    • Fill crater before breaking arc
    • Use backstep technique at the end of welds
    • Pause at the end to allow crater to fill
    • Use "crater fill" technique: reduce current at end
  4. Repair Existing Cracks:
    • Remove crack completely by grinding
    • Verify crack removal with dye penetrant testing
    • Preheat area before repair welding
    • Use low hydrogen process for repair
    • Consider stress relief after repair

Slag Inclusions

Symptoms:

  • Slag trapped within weld metal
  • Visible as dark spots on radiograph
  • Often in lines or clusters
  • May not be visible on surface

Causes:

  1. Inadequate Cleaning Between Passes: Trapped slag from previous pass
  2. Poor Electrode Manipulation: Not allowing slag to float
  3. Travel Speed Too Fast: Slag overtaken by weld pool
  4. Too Low Amperage: Insufficient fluidity of weld pool
  5. Poor Joint Design: Tight angles trapping slag
  6. Improper Electrode Angle: Pushing slag ahead of arc

Solutions:

  1. Proper Interpass Cleaning:
    • Remove all slag completely before next pass
    • Use chipping hammer to break slag
    • Follow with wire brush cleaning
    • Pay special attention to corners and edges
    • Inspect thoroughly before continuing
  2. Adjust Welding Technique:
    • Use proper weave pattern allowing slag to float
    • Maintain proper travel speed
    • Hold correct work angle
    • Ensure adequate fluidity of weld pool
  3. Joint Design Considerations:
    • Provide adequate access for cleaning
    • Avoid extremely tight angles
    • Use joint designs that facilitate slag removal
  4. Parameter Adjustments:
    • Increase amperage within electrode range
    • Ensure proper arc length
    • Consider electrode type with better slag detachability

Incomplete Penetration

Symptoms:

  • Root of joint not completely fused
  • Gap or void at the root
  • Visible as dark line in radiograph at center of weld
  • Reduced strength at root

Causes:

  1. Insufficient Heat Input: Amperage too low
  2. Improper Joint Design:
    • Root face too thick
    • Root opening too tight
    • Included angle too narrow
  3. Electrode Selection: Inadequate penetration characteristics
  4. Poor Technique: Not focusing arc on root
  5. Travel Speed Too Fast: Insufficient time for penetration
  6. Misalignment: Poor fit-up creating uneven gap

Solutions:

  1. Adjust Welding Parameters:
    • Increase amperage for better penetration
    • Use correct polarity (DCEP for maximum penetration)
    • Select appropriate electrode size for joint
  2. Improve Joint Design:
    • Ensure adequate root opening (typically 1/16" to 3/32")
    • Use proper bevel angle (60-75° included angle)
    • Reduce root face thickness (1/16" or less)
    • Consider backing strip or backing weld
  3. Electrode Selection:
    • Use E6010 or E6011 for root passes
    • Select smaller diameter electrode for root pass
    • Choose electrodes with deep penetration characteristics
  4. Welding Technique:
    • Focus arc on root of joint
    • Use slight side-to-side motion for wider root passes
    • Maintain proper travel speed (slower for better penetration)
    • For open root, maintain visible keyhole

Equipment and Operational Issues

Electrode Sticking

Symptoms:

  • Electrode fuses to workpiece during arc initiation
  • Frequent sticking during welding
  • Difficulty restarting after electrode sticks

Causes:

  1. Amperage Too Low: Insufficient heat to establish arc
  2. Improper Starting Technique: Holding electrode against work too long
  3. Damp Electrodes: Moisture affecting coating performance
  4. Long Arc Length: Then touching workpiece
  5. Machine Anti-Stick Feature Off: Some machines have anti-stick settings

Solutions:

  1. Adjust Amperage:
    • Increase amperage to appropriate level
    • Use minimum amperage recommended for electrode size
    • Consider moving to smaller electrode if needed
  2. Improve Starting Technique:
    • Use quick scratch motion like striking a match
    • Lift electrode immediately after contact
    • Practice on scrap material
  3. When Electrode Sticks:
    • Release electrode immediately (don't pull)
    • Turn off machine or short-circuit switch
    • Twist electrode gently to break free
    • Redeposit wasted electrode before continuing
  4. Electrode Handling:
    • Ensure electrodes are dry
    • Store in appropriate conditions
    • Try different electrode type if problems persist

Welding Machine Problems

Common Symptoms:

  • Machine won't power on
  • Inconsistent arc or amperage fluctuations
  • Overheating
  • Circuit breaker trips
  • Poor arc performance despite proper technique

Power Supply Issues:

  • Insufficient input power
  • Improper voltage settings
  • Loose connections
  • Undersized extension cords
  • Generator issues (if applicable)

Machine Component Issues:

  • Faulty control board
  • Cooling fan failure
  • Damaged internal components
  • Dirty internal components

Connection Issues:

  • Loose welding cables
  • Improper grounding
  • Damaged electrode holder
  • Poor work clamp connection

Power Supply Solutions:

  • Verify input power meets machine requirements
  • Check voltage settings on dual-voltage machines
  • Use proper size extension cord (if needed)
  • Ensure generator has sufficient capacity
  • Avoid sharing circuits with other high-draw equipment

Connection Solutions:

  • Inspect and tighten all cable connections
  • Clean work clamp contact area
  • Check for cable damage or excessive wear
  • Replace damaged electrode holders or work clamps

Maintenance Solutions:

  • Clean machine regularly with compressed air
  • Check for blocked air vents
  • Follow manufacturer's maintenance schedule
  • Let machine cool if duty cycle is exceeded

When to Seek Professional Service:

  • Error codes displayed
  • Burning smell from machine
  • Visible internal damage
  • Persistent problems after basic troubleshooting
  • Electrical hazards (sparking, shocking)

Advanced Troubleshooting Scenarios

Challenging Materials

High-Carbon and Alloy Steels:

Common Issues:

  • Cracking in heat-affected zone
  • Excessive hardness
  • Poor fusion

Solutions:

  • Proper preheat (400-700°F depending on carbon content)
  • Use low hydrogen electrodes (E7018, E8018)
  • Maintain interpass temperature
  • Slow post-weld cooling (cover with insulating blanket)
  • Post-weld heat treatment when possible
  • Multiple, smaller passes rather than single large beads

Cast Iron:

Common Issues:

  • Cracking during or after welding
  • Hard, brittle welds that can't be machined
  • Poor fusion at edges

Solutions:

  • Proper preheat (500-1200°F depending on size and complexity)
  • Use nickel electrodes (ENi-CI) for machinability
  • Short, small stringer beads (no weaving)
  • Peen each pass while still hot
  • Very slow cooling after welding
  • Use lowest practical amperage (30-40% less than steel)

Stainless Steel:

Common Issues:

  • Excessive distortion
  • Sensitization (chromium carbide precipitation)
  • Hot cracking
  • Sugar (discoloration at heat-affected zone)

Solutions:

  • Use lower heat input than carbon steel
  • Select proper electrode (308L for 304, 316L for 316)
  • Use stringer beads rather than weaves
  • Control interpass temperature (under 300°F)
  • Strong fixturing to control distortion
  • Remove heat tint with stainless steel pickling paste

Positional Welding Challenges

Vertical Welding Issues:

Common Issues:

  • Molten metal sagging or running
  • Poor bead formation
  • Incomplete fusion at toes
  • Difficult puddle control

Solutions for Vertical-Up:

  • Reduce amperage (70-85% of flat position)
  • Use appropriate manipulation pattern:
    • Triangle/Christmas Tree pattern
    • J-weave with pause at top
    • Step and pause technique
  • Use smaller diameter electrode
  • Focus on controlling the leading edge of the puddle
  • Maintain tight arc length

Solutions for Vertical-Down:

  • Only suitable for thin materials
  • Higher travel speed than vertical-up
  • Focus on leading edge of puddle
  • Maintain very short arc length
  • Use 15-20° push angle

Overhead Welding Issues:

Common Issues:

  • Molten metal dropping
  • Difficult arc control
  • Excessive spatter
  • Operator fatigue affecting quality

Solutions:

  • Reduce amperage (70-80% of flat position)
  • Use smaller diameter electrode
  • Keep weld pool small (maximum 3× electrode diameter)
  • Use stringer beads or very tight weave
  • Maintain shortest possible arc length
  • Higher travel speed to keep puddle small
  • Consider whip technique to control puddle
  • Position body for maximum stability and comfort

Root Pass Challenges

Common Root Pass Issues:

  • Incomplete penetration
  • Excessive penetration (burn-through)
  • Concave root surface
  • Lack of fusion at root
  • Root porosity

For Open Root Joints:

  • Electrode Selection:
    • E6010 or E6011 for deep penetration
    • 3/32" or 1/8" diameter typical
  • Key Technique Adjustments:
    • Maintain visible keyhole at leading edge of puddle
    • Keyhole should be approximately 2/3 electrode diameter size
    • Rock or weave slightly to ensure side wall fusion
    • Monitor puddle carefully for signs of burn-through
    • Adjust travel speed to control keyhole size
  • For Excessive Penetration/Burn-Through:
    • Reduce amperage
    • Increase travel speed
    • Use tighter root gap
    • Consider using landing/root face
  • For Incomplete Penetration:
    • Increase amperage
    • Slow travel speed
    • Ensure proper root gap (typically 1/16" to 3/32")
    • Reduce root face thickness
    • Focus arc on root

For Joints with Backing:

  • Ensure backing is clean and properly fitted
  • Control heat input to prevent excessive melt-through
  • Focus arc at the junction of joint and backing
  • Use consistent travel speed
  • E7018 can be used as alternative to E6010/E6011
