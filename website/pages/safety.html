Safety First

Welding involves multiple hazards that can cause serious injury or death if proper safety precautions are not followed. Never compromise on safety equipment or procedures. This guide provides general safety information but is not a substitute for formal training or manufacturer instructions.

Understanding the potential hazards in welding is the first step in preventing injuries and maintaining a safe work environment.

Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

Eye and Face Protection

Welding Helmet

The welding helmet is your primary protection against arc radiation and spatter.

  • Shade Selection: For stick welding (SMAW)
    • 60-160 amps: Shade #10
    • 160-250 amps: Shade #12
    • 250-550 amps: Shade #14
  • Types of Helmets:
    • Fixed Shade: Traditional, reliable, inexpensive
    • Auto-Darkening: Convenient, allows positioning with helmet down
      • Reaction time: 1/10,000 - 1/25,000 second
      • Adjustable sensitivity and delay settings
      • Multiple shade settings (often 9-13)
      • Battery powered (some with solar assist)
  • Maintenance:
    • Regularly clean lens and cover plates
    • Replace scratched or damaged lenses
    • Check headgear adjustment mechanisms
    • Inspect for cracks or damage
    • For auto-darkening: check batteries regularly

Safety Glasses

Always wear safety glasses under your welding helmet and when grinding, chipping, or preparing materials.

  • ANSI Z87.1 rated impact-resistant glasses
  • Side shields for protection from peripheral hazards
  • Clear lenses when under helmet, tinted when appropriate for other work
  • Consider prescription safety glasses if needed

Never look at a welding arc without proper eye protection, even for a moment. Arc flash injuries can develop hours after exposure and can cause significant pain and temporary or permanent eye damage.

Respiratory Protection

Welding fumes contain metal oxides and other compounds that can cause both short-term and long-term health effects.

Types of Respiratory Protection

  • Disposable Particulate Respirators:
    • N95 or P100 rated (minimum N95)
    • Suitable for short-term, light-duty welding
    • Must have proper fit and seal
    • Not suitable for confined spaces
  • Half-Mask Air-Purifying Respirators:
    • Reusable design with replaceable filters
    • Better seal than disposable masks
    • Use P100 filters for welding fumes
    • Requires proper fit testing
  • Powered Air-Purifying Respirators (PAPR):
    • Battery-powered air delivery
    • Integrated with welding helmet in some models
    • Provides positive pressure (highest protection)
    • More comfortable for extended use
    • Higher cost but better protection

When to Use Respiratory Protection

  • Always when working with:
    • Stainless steel (contains chromium, nickel)
    • Galvanized steel (zinc coating)
    • Materials with coatings (paint, primer, etc.)
    • Any material that produces toxic fumes
  • In confined or poorly ventilated spaces
  • During extended welding sessions
  • When local exhaust ventilation is inadequate

Respiratory protection should be used in conjunction with proper ventilation, not as a substitute for it. When possible, position yourself to avoid breathing the fume plume.

Body Protection

Protective Clothing

  • Welding Jacket or Coat:
    • Flame-resistant material (leather, treated cotton, or FR-rated fabric)
    • Coverage for arms and torso
    • Closed front with no pockets or covered pockets
    • Cuffs to prevent spark entry
  • Pants:
    • No cuffs (can catch sparks)
    • No frayed edges
    • Flame-resistant material
    • Cover pant legs over boots (no tucking in)
  • Apron:
    • Additional protection for high-spatter applications
    • Typically leather
    • Covers from chest to below knees

Gloves

  • Material: Leather (typically cowhide or elk skin)
  • Design:
    • Gauntlet-style to protect wrists and forearms
    • Heat-resistant
    • Reinforced palm and fingers
    • Welted seams (internal stitching)
  • Fit:
    • Snug but not restrictive
    • Allows for electrode manipulation
    • Appropriate size for hand dimensions
  • Care:
    • Replace when worn or damaged
    • Keep dry and clean
    • Check regularly for holes or worn areas

Footwear

  • High-top leather boots with safety toes
  • Oil-resistant soles
  • Metatarsal guards for heavy spatter environments
  • No exposed laces that can catch sparks
  • Pants should cover tops of boots

Head and Ear Protection

  • Welding cap or FR beanie to protect scalp and hair
  • FR hood for overhead welding
  • Earplugs or earmuffs in high-noise environments
  • Keep hair contained to prevent fire hazard

Never wear synthetic fabrics like polyester or nylon while welding. These materials can melt and adhere to skin, causing severe burns. Cotton, wool, leather, and specially treated FR fabrics are safer options.

Workspace and Environmental Safety

Ventilation and Air Quality

Proper ventilation is essential for removing welding fumes and maintaining breathable air quality.

Types of Ventilation

  • Natural Ventilation:
    • Open doors and windows
    • Cross-ventilation when possible
    • Only suitable for light, occasional welding in large spaces
  • Local Exhaust Ventilation (LEV):
    • Fume extraction arms positioned 6-12 inches from arc
    • Captures fumes at source before reaching breathing zone
    • Most effective form of ventilation
    • Adjustable positioning to follow work
  • General Mechanical Ventilation:
    • Roof or wall mounted exhaust fans
    • Air replacement systems
    • Creates overall airflow through workspace
    • Less effective than local exhaust for fume control
  • Portable Fume Extractors:
    • Self-contained filtration units
    • Flexibility for different work locations
    • Various filter options (mechanical, electrostatic, activated carbon)
    • Good for smaller shops or occasional work

Ventilation Considerations

  • Position yourself so fumes move away from breathing zone
  • Never position head directly above weld
  • Ensure makeup air (replacement air) is clean
  • Increase ventilation for confined spaces, toxic materials
  • Remember that some fumes are heavier than air (position extraction accordingly)
  • Follow OSHA regulations for specific materials

Never weld in a confined space without proper ventilation and monitoring. Welding can rapidly deplete oxygen and create toxic atmospheres that can cause asphyxiation or death.

Fire Prevention and Control

Welding creates sparks, slag, and heat that can ignite materials up to 35 feet away from the work area.

Fire Hazard Inspection

Before welding, inspect the area within a 35-foot radius for:

  • Flammable liquids, gases, or vapors
  • Paper, cardboard, rags, or other combustibles
  • Openings in floors, walls, or ceilings where sparks could travel
  • Hidden areas where material could smolder undetected
  • Ducts or conveyor systems that could transport sparks

Fire Prevention Measures

  • Remove Combustibles: Clear all flammable materials from work area
  • Cover Immovable Combustibles: Use flame-resistant blankets or guards
  • Shield Openings: Cover floor drains, wall gaps, etc.
  • Wet Down: Dampen wooden floors or other surfaces when appropriate
  • Fire Watch: Have someone monitor for fires during and after welding
  • Hot Work Permit: Follow formal procedures in regulated environments

Fire Suppression Equipment

  • Fire Extinguishers:
    • Minimum 10 lb. ABC dry chemical extinguisher
    • Located within 30 feet of work area
    • Inspected and current
    • All workers trained in proper use
  • Fire Blankets: For small fires or to smother flames on clothing
  • Sand Buckets: Alternative for metal fires
  • Water Hose: Only for appropriate fire types (not electrical or metal fires)

Post-Welding Fire Watch

  • Monitor work area for at least 30 minutes after welding ceases
  • Check behind walls, below floors, and above ceilings
  • Look for smoke, embers, heat, or unusual odors
  • For high-risk areas, extend monitoring to 60 minutes or more
  • Remember that fires can develop hours after welding is complete

Never use oxygen to "freshen" air in confined spaces or to blow dust off clothing. Oxygen-enriched atmospheres dramatically increase fire risks—materials that normally resist ignition can burn explosively in oxygen-rich environments.

Electrical Safety

Electric shock is one of the most serious and immediate hazards in welding, capable of causing severe injury or death.

Primary Voltage Safety (Input Power)

  • Ensure proper grounding of welding machine
  • Use proper plug configurations without adapters
  • Follow manufacturer's installation instructions
  • Maintain and inspect power cords regularly
  • Never operate equipment with damaged cords
  • Use proper extension cords rated for the amperage
  • Keep connections dry and protected

Secondary Voltage Safety (Welding Circuit)

  • Inspect electrode holder and work clamp for damage
  • Ensure proper insulation on all cables
  • Never use welding cables with damaged insulation
  • Make solid connections to workpiece
  • Connect work clamp as close to weld as practical
  • Never coil cables around your body
  • Keep cables organized and out of walkways

Personal Electrical Safety

  • Keep work area dry; never weld in wet conditions
  • Use dry gloves without holes
  • Insulate yourself from the work and ground
    • Use dry insulation material (rubber mats, plywood)
    • Avoid contact with conductive surfaces
    • Wear dry boots with insulated soles
  • Avoid simultaneous contact with workpiece and electrode holder
  • Change electrodes with dry gloves only
  • Turn off machine when not in use or when changing connections

Maintenance and Inspection

  • Regular inspection of all electrical components
  • Follow lockout/tagout procedures when servicing equipment
  • Keep interior of welding machine clean and dry
  • Only qualified personnel should service internal components
  • Test GFCI protection regularly if installed

Even secondary welding voltage (typically 60-80 volts open circuit) can be lethal under the right conditions, especially in confined spaces, wet environments, or when the welder is sweating heavily. Treat all electrical equipment with appropriate caution.

Special Situations and Environments

Confined Space Welding

Confined spaces present multiple heightened hazards, including atmospheric dangers, restricted movement, and limited escape routes.

Definition of Confined Space

A confined space typically has these characteristics:

  • Limited or restricted entry and exit
  • Not designed for continuous occupancy
  • Large enough for a worker to enter and perform work
  • Examples: tanks, vessels, silos, pits, manholes, pipelines

Hazards in Confined Spaces

  • Atmospheric Hazards:
    • Oxygen depletion or enrichment
    • Buildup of welding fumes
    • Accumulation of shielding gases
    • Presence of flammable or toxic gases
  • Physical Hazards:
    • Limited movement and working positions
    • Restricted vision
    • Difficult escape in emergency
    • Heat buildup
    • Increased electrical hazards

Required Safety Measures

  • Permits and Procedures:
    • Formal confined space entry permit
    • Documented procedures
    • Authorized personnel only
  • Atmospheric Testing:
    • Test for oxygen levels (19.5% - 23.5% safe range)
    • Test for flammable gases (below 10% of LEL)
    • Test for toxic gases (CO, H2S, etc.)
    • Continuous monitoring during work
  • Ventilation:
    • Mechanical ventilation required
    • Minimum 2000 CFM fresh air per welder
    • Exhaust from bottom if gases are heavier than air
    • Ensure fresh air supply is not contaminated
  • Attendant:
    • Dedicated attendant outside the space
    • Maintains constant communication
    • Authorized to order evacuation
    • Can summon emergency assistance
  • Rescue Equipment:
    • Retrieval systems (harness, lifeline)
    • Emergency extraction equipment
    • Self-contained breathing apparatus for rescuers
    • Practiced rescue procedures

Electrical Considerations

  • Use DC welding current when possible
  • Use automatic voltage reduction devices
  • Insulate worker from workpiece and ground
  • Use vapor-proof lights for illumination
  • Keep power source outside confined space

Never enter a confined space without proper training, equipment, and procedures. More than 60% of confined space fatalities occur among would-be rescuers who enter unprepared. Always follow proper confined space protocols.

Elevated Work Areas

Welding at heights combines standard welding hazards with fall risks and creates unique safety challenges.

Fall Protection

  • Personal Fall Arrest Systems:
    • Full-body harness (not belt)
    • Shock-absorbing lanyard
    • Secure anchor points
    • Proper calculation of fall distances
  • Guardrail Systems:
    • Top rail, mid-rail, and toe board
    • Properly secured and inspected
  • Safety Nets: When other systems are impractical
  • Designated Safety Monitor: For special circumstances

Special Fire Prevention

  • Be aware of what is below work area
  • Use spark/slag catchers (fire blankets)
  • Ensure sparks cannot fall through openings
  • Position fire watch below when necessary
  • Keep fire extinguisher at elevated work area

Equipment Considerations

  • Secure all tools and equipment
  • Use tool lanyards or tethers
  • Consider welding machine location and cable management
  • Ensure stable working position
  • Clear workspace of trip hazards

Outdoor Welding

Outdoor environments introduce additional variables and hazards to the welding process.

Weather Considerations

  • Rain and Moisture:
    • Never weld in rain or standing water
    • Protect equipment from moisture
    • Use shelters or tents as needed
    • Heightened electrical shock risk
  • Wind:
    • Use windscreens to protect arc and shielding
    • Secure materials against unexpected movement
    • Adjust gas flow rates if using GMAW/FCAW
    • Be aware of spark travel in wind
  • Temperature Extremes:
    • Cold: Protect against hypothermia, consider material brittleness
    • Heat: Protection against heat stress, increased breaks
    • Dress in layers appropriate to conditions

Environmental Protections

  • Contain slag and debris
  • Protect waterways from contamination
  • Appropriate disposal of stubs and waste
  • Be aware of fire risk to surrounding vegetation
  • Consider wildlife and habitat impacts

Public Safety

  • Use screens to protect passersby from arc flash
  • Establish work zone with barriers when needed
  • Post appropriate warning signs
  • Secure equipment during breaks
  • Be aware of curious onlookers, especially children

Welding-Specific First Aid

Despite best prevention efforts, accidents can happen. Knowing how to respond to welding-specific injuries can minimize damage and expedite recovery.

This information is not a substitute for professional medical training or treatment. Always seek professional medical attention for significant injuries or when in doubt.

Eye Injuries

Arc Flash (Photokeratitis / "Welder's Flash")

  • Symptoms: Delayed pain (4-12 hours), redness, tearing, gritty feeling, light sensitivity
  • First Aid:
    • Do NOT rub eyes
    • Apply cold compresses (do not place ice directly on eyes)
    • Use artificial tears for lubrication
    • Take over-the-counter pain relievers if needed
    • Rest in dark room
    • Seek medical attention if severe or not improving

Foreign Objects

  • Symptoms: Immediate pain, tearing, feeling of something in eye
  • First Aid:
    • Do NOT rub eye
    • Flush with clean water or eyewash solution
    • Do NOT attempt to remove embedded objects
    • Cover eye with shield (not pressure patch)
    • Seek immediate medical attention for embedded objects

Burns

Thermal Burns

  • First Degree (redness):
    • Cool with cold water for 10-15 minutes
    • Cover with clean, dry bandage
    • Over-the-counter pain relievers if needed
  • Second Degree (blisters):
    • Cool with cold water for 15-20 minutes
    • Do NOT break blisters
    • Cover with sterile, non-stick bandage
    • Seek medical attention
  • Third Degree (white/charred):
    • Call emergency services immediately
    • Do NOT remove clothing stuck to burn
    • Cover with clean, dry cloth
    • Monitor for shock

Chemical Burns (from flux or cleaning agents)

  • Remove contaminated clothing
  • Flush with large amounts of water for at least 20 minutes
  • Do NOT apply neutralizing agents
  • Seek medical attention

Electrical Injuries

  • First Response:
    • Do NOT touch victim if still in contact with electrical source
    • Turn off power source if possible
    • Use non-conductive material to separate victim from source if necessary
    • Check for breathing and pulse
    • Begin CPR if needed and if trained
    • Call emergency services immediately
  • After Safety Secured:
    • Check for entry and exit wounds
    • Treat burns as described above
    • Monitor for shock (pale skin, rapid breathing, weakness)
    • Keep victim warm and still
    • All electrical shock victims should receive medical evaluation

Fume Inhalation

  • Symptoms: Coughing, shortness of breath, irritation, dizziness, nausea
  • First Aid:
    • Move victim to fresh air immediately
    • Loosen tight clothing
    • If breathing is difficult, seek immediate medical attention
    • If metal fume fever develops (flu-like symptoms appearing hours after exposure), encourage rest and fluids
    • Seek medical attention if symptoms are severe or persistent

Consider taking a welding-specific first aid course. Organizations like the American Welding Society and many technical colleges offer specialized training that covers the unique injuries associated with welding and metal fabrication.
