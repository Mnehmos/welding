Electrode Basics

The electrode is the heart of stick welding. Selecting the right electrode for your application greatly impacts weld quality, efficiency, and the overall success of your project.

Anatomy of an Electrode

A stick welding electrode consists of:

  • Core Wire: The metal rod that melts to become the weld metal
  • Flux Coating: The outer layer that:
    • Creates a protective gas shield
    • Forms slag to protect the cooling weld
    • Adds alloying elements to the weld
    • Stabilizes the arc
    • Controls bead shape and characteristics
  • Bare End: The uncoated portion that connects to the electrode holder

Electrode Classification System

The American Welding Society (AWS) classification system provides a standardized method for identifying electrode characteristics. For carbon steel electrodes, the system uses an "E" prefix followed by a four or five-digit number:

E XXXX-X

  • E - Indicates an electrode
  • First 2-3 digits - Tensile strength (in ksi or 1,000 pounds per square inch)
  • Next-to-last digit - Welding positions (1=all positions, 2=flat/horizontal only)
  • Last digit - Coating type, current type, and operating characteristics
  • Suffix - Additional characteristics (if present)

Examples:

  • E6010: 60,000 psi tensile strength, all positions, DC+ only, deep penetration
  • E7018: 70,000 psi tensile strength, all positions, low hydrogen, DC+ or AC
  • E6013: 60,000 psi tensile strength, all positions, AC or DC either polarity
  • E7024: 70,000 psi tensile strength, flat/horizontal only, high iron powder, AC or DC

General rule: Use the largest diameter electrode that's practical for the position and joint configuration. Larger electrodes typically provide higher deposition rates and efficiency.

Current Type and Polarity

Different electrodes require specific current types and polarities:

  • DCEP (DC+): Deeper penetration, often used for thick materials and root passes
  • DCEN (DC-): Lower penetration, higher deposition rate, often used for thin materials
  • AC: Moderate penetration, helps prevent arc blow, typically less expensive equipment

The correct current and polarity are specified by the electrode's last digit:

Last Digit Current Type Characteristics
0 DCEP only Deep penetration, cellulose sodium coating
1 AC or DCEP Deep penetration, cellulose potassium coating
2 AC or DCEN Medium penetration, titania sodium coating
3 AC, DCEP, or DCEN Light penetration, titania potassium coating
4 AC, DCEP, or DCEN Iron powder, titania coating, high deposition
5 DCEP only Low hydrogen sodium coating
6 AC or DCEP Low hydrogen potassium coating
7 AC or DC (both polarities) Iron powder, low hydrogen coating
8 AC or DCEP Iron powder, low hydrogen coating, improved properties

Using an electrode with incorrect current type or polarity can lead to poor arc stability, excessive spatter, porosity, and poor bead formation.

Common Electrode Types

E6010

E6010 is a high-cellulose sodium electrode known for its deep penetration and ability to weld through rust, dirt, and paint.

Characteristics:

  • Tensile Strength: 60,000 psi
  • Positions: All positions
  • Current: DCEP only
  • Penetration: Very deep
  • Slag: Light, easy to remove
  • Arc Force: Forceful, digger arc
  • Spatter: Moderate to high

Best Applications:

  • Root passes on pipe
  • Open root joints
  • Dirty or rusty steel
  • Structural steel fabrication
  • Field repairs where cleaning is difficult
  • Applications requiring deep penetration

Limitations:

  • Not for use with AC power
  • Rougher bead appearance
  • Higher skill level required
  • Not for critical applications requiring X-ray quality without proper technique

Tips for Use:

  • Maintain a short arc length
  • Use a slight drag angle (5-15°)
  • Watch for keyhole in open root applications
  • Store in dry conditions (though less sensitive than low hydrogen types)

E6011

E6011 is similar to E6010 but can be used with AC power sources, making it more versatile for equipment limitations.

Characteristics:

  • Tensile Strength: 60,000 psi
  • Positions: All positions
  • Current: AC or DCEP
  • Penetration: Deep
  • Slag: Light, easy to remove
  • Arc Force: Strong, forceful
  • Spatter: Moderate to high

Best Applications:

  • Similar to E6010 but when only AC is available
  • Root passes on pipe with AC power
  • Field repairs with AC generators
  • Rusty or dirty steel
  • Maintenance and repair work

Limitations:

  • Slightly less penetration than E6010 on AC
  • Rougher bead appearance
  • Higher skill level required

Tips for Use:

  • On AC, use slightly higher amperage than DC equivalent
  • Maintain shorter arc length
  • Use similar techniques to E6010

E6013

E6013 is a general-purpose electrode with a smooth arc, easy slag removal, and minimal spatter, making it ideal for beginners.

Characteristics:

  • Tensile Strength: 60,000 psi
  • Positions: All positions
  • Current: AC or DC (either polarity)
  • Penetration: Light to moderate
  • Slag: Forms quickly, easy to remove
  • Arc Force: Smooth, soft
  • Spatter: Minimal

Best Applications:

  • Sheet metal work
  • Thin gauge steel
  • Light structural work
  • Auto body repair
  • Home and hobby projects
  • Training and education

Limitations:

  • Limited penetration
  • Not for critical structural applications
  • Not for thick materials without multiple passes
  • Not for code work requiring specific properties

Tips for Use:

  • Works well with lower amperage settings
  • Can maintain slightly longer arc length
  • Great for learning arc control
  • Produces attractive beads with consistent ripple pattern

E7018

E7018 is a low-hydrogen electrode with iron powder in the coating, producing high-quality, strong welds with excellent mechanical properties.

Characteristics:

  • Tensile Strength: 70,000 psi
  • Positions: All positions
  • Current: AC or DCEP (preferred)
  • Penetration: Moderate
  • Slag: Moderate, easy to remove
  • Arc Force: Smooth, medium
  • Spatter: Low

Best Applications:

  • Structural steel fabrication
  • Pressure vessel welding
  • Bridge construction
  • Heavy equipment manufacturing
  • Fill and cap passes on pipe
  • Critical applications requiring high-quality deposits

Limitations:

  • Requires proper storage and handling (low hydrogen)
  • Less effective on dirty or rusty material
  • Not recommended for root passes without backing
  • Less operator appeal for open root

Tips for Use:

  • Store in heated rod ovens (250-300°F) after opening
  • Use drags angle of 5-10°
  • Works well with stringer beads or weave techniques
  • Produces a flatter bead profile than cellulosic electrodes

E7018 electrodes absorb moisture from the air, which can lead to porosity and hydrogen cracking. Always store opened electrodes in a rod oven and discard electrodes that have been exposed to moisture for extended periods.

E7024

E7024 is a high-deposition iron powder electrode designed for flat and horizontal positions, providing fast filling of joints with minimal skill required.

Characteristics:

  • Tensile Strength: 70,000 psi
  • Positions: Flat and horizontal only
  • Current: AC or DC (either polarity)
  • Penetration: Light
  • Slag: Heavy, easy to remove
  • Arc Force: Smooth, soft
  • Spatter: Very low
  • Deposition Rate: Very high (up to 3x other electrodes)

Best Applications:

  • Flat and horizontal fillet welds
  • Fill passes on thick material
  • Production welding requiring high deposition
  • Surfacing and buildup operations
  • Single-pass applications where penetration isn't critical

Limitations:

  • Limited to flat and horizontal positions
  • Light penetration limits use for critical joints
  • Not suitable for root passes
  • Not for vertical or overhead welding

Tips for Use:

  • Position the electrode so the heavy flux coating is on the bottom
  • Use a longer arc length than with other electrodes
  • Minimal manipulation needed - let the electrode "drag" along the joint
  • Great for long, straight welds due to high deposition and operator comfort

Specialty Electrodes

E308 - Stainless Steel

E308 is one of the most common stainless steel electrodes, primarily used for welding 304 stainless steel.

Characteristics:

  • Material: 19% Chromium, 9% Nickel
  • Positions: Varies by suffix (L for all positions)
  • Current: Typically DC+
  • Penetration: Moderate
  • Corrosion Resistance: Good

Variants:

  • E308-16: All positions, lime coating, AC or DC+
  • E308-15: All positions, lime coating, DC+ only
  • E308L-16: Low carbon version for better resistance to intergranular corrosion

Applications:

  • Welding 304 stainless steel
  • Food processing equipment
  • Chemical containers
  • Architectural fabrication

ENi-CI - Cast Iron Electrode

ENi-CI is a pure nickel electrode specifically designed for cast iron welding, providing a machinable, crack-resistant deposit.

Characteristics:

  • Material: ~97% Nickel
  • Positions: All positions
  • Current: DC-
  • Penetration: Light
  • Machinability: Excellent

Applications:

  • Engine blocks and heads
  • Machinery bases
  • Pump housings
  • Areas requiring machining after welding
  • Thin section cast iron

Tips for Use:

  • Use low amperage settings - typically 40% less than carbon steel
  • Short arc length
  • Stringer beads only - no weaving
  • Preheat cast iron (500-1200°F)
  • Slow cooling after welding

E4043 - Aluminum Electrode

E4043 is an aluminum electrode with silicon alloying, designed to overcome the challenges of stick welding aluminum.

Characteristics:

  • Material: Aluminum-Silicon alloy
  • Positions: All positions, but challenging
  • Current: DC+
  • Penetration: Light
  • Flux: Heavy coating

Applications:

  • Repair of aluminum castings
  • General aluminum fabrication
  • Field repairs where other processes aren't available
  • Non-critical aluminum joints

Tips for Use:

  • Higher skill level required
  • Preheat to 300-400°F
  • Clean thoroughly - aluminum oxide inhibits fusion
  • Strike arc on a separate piece of aluminum then quickly move to joint
  • High travel speed to prevent burnthrough

Aluminum stick welding is challenging. For most aluminum applications, TIG or MIG welding is preferred. Use stick only when other processes aren't available.

Hard-Facing Electrodes

Hard-facing electrodes deposit wear-resistant materials to extend the service life of components subject to abrasion, impact, or other wear mechanisms.

Common Types:

  • Chromium Carbide: For severe abrasion resistance
  • Manganese: For impact resistance
  • Martensitic: General purpose wear resistance
  • Buildup: For dimensional restoration before hard-facing

Applications:

  • Excavator buckets and teeth
  • Crusher parts
  • Agricultural implements
  • Mining equipment
  • Wear plates
  • Concrete mixer paddles

Tips for Use:

  • Clean base metal thoroughly
  • Preheat according to manufacturer's recommendations
  • Use stringer beads for most applications
  • Control heat input to prevent cracking
  • Follow manufacturer's recommendations for overlapping beads
  • Consider buildup layers before applying hard-facing

Electrode Selection Guide

Electrode Selection Process

Follow this step-by-step process to select the optimal electrode for your application:

  1. Identify Base Metal: Determine the material type and thickness
  2. Consider Joint Design: Root access, fit-up, position
  3. Evaluate Required Properties: Strength, corrosion resistance, etc.
  4. Assess Welding Conditions: Indoor/outdoor, power source availability
  5. Evaluate Position: Flat, horizontal, vertical, overhead
  6. Consider Welder Skill Level: Beginner, intermediate, advanced
  7. Review Code Requirements: If applicable
  8. Match Power Source Capabilities: AC, DC, amperage range

Special Application Considerations

Code Requirements:

Many welding codes specify particular electrode types:

  • AWS D1.1 (Structural Welding Code - Steel): Often requires low-hydrogen electrodes (E7018) for critical applications
  • ASME Section IX (Boiler and Pressure Vessel Code): Specifies electrode F-numbers for procedure qualification
  • API 1104 (Pipeline Welding): Often uses E6010 for root passes and E7018 for fill/cap

Economic Considerations:

Total welding costs include more than just electrode price:

  • Deposition Efficiency: Higher deposition rate electrodes (E7024) may be more economical despite higher price
  • Operating Factor: Ease of use affects productivity
  • Defect Rate: Repair costs can outweigh initial electrode savings
  • Storage Requirements: Low hydrogen electrodes require special storage (rod ovens)

Special Environment Considerations:

  • High Moisture/Humidity: Consider E6010/E6011, which are less sensitive to moisture
  • Windy Conditions: E6010/E6011 provide better shield gas protection in wind
  • Limited Access: Smaller diameter electrodes or special coating types
  • Limited Power Available: E6013 works well with lower amperage

Electrode Handling and Storage

Understanding Moisture Effects

Moisture in electrode coatings can lead to several weld defects:

  • Porosity: Gas bubbles trapped in the weld
  • Increased Spatter: Explosive release of hydrogen gas
  • Hydrogen Cracking: Hydrogen-induced cold cracking in the weld or HAZ
  • Arc Instability: Erratic arc behavior
  • Reduced Mechanical Properties: Lower strength and ductility

Some electrode types are more susceptible to moisture damage than others:

  • High Susceptibility: E7018, E7016, and other low hydrogen electrodes
  • Medium Susceptibility: E7024, E7028, iron powder electrodes
  • Low Susceptibility: E6010, E6011, cellulosic electrodes
  • Very Low Susceptibility: E6013, titanium dioxide electrodes

Proper Storage Requirements

Electrode Type Unopened Package Opened Package Maximum Exposure Time
Low Hydrogen (E7018, E7016) Dry area, sealed package Rod oven at 250-300°F 4 hours at ambient conditions
Iron Powder (E7024, E7028) Dry area, sealed package Rod oven at 150-200°F 8 hours at ambient conditions
Cellulosic (E6010, E6011) Dry area Dry area 24+ hours (less critical)
Rutile (E6013, E7014) Dry area Dry area 24+ hours (less critical)
Stainless Steel Dry area, sealed package Rod oven at 200-250°F 8 hours at ambient conditions

Reconditioning Electrodes

If low hydrogen electrodes have been exposed to moisture beyond the recommended time, they can sometimes be reconditioned:

  1. Baking Procedure:
    • Place electrodes in oven at 500-800°F (per manufacturer's recommendations)
    • Bake for 1-2 hours
    • Reduce temperature to 250-300°F for storage
  2. Important Notes:
    • Some electrodes can only be reconditioned once
    • Never recondition electrodes that show visible coating damage
    • Follow manufacturer's specific recommendations
    • Record reconditioning cycles for quality control

When in doubt, discard questionable electrodes. The cost of replacing electrodes is minimal compared to the potential cost of weld failures and repairs.

Field Handling Tips

  • Portable Rod Canisters: Use insulated canisters with desiccant for short-term field storage
  • Small Batches: Only remove as many electrodes as can be used within the exposure limit
  • Electrode Quivers: Keep on welder's person to maintain body heat and reduce moisture exposure
  • Portable Rod Ovens: For critical applications, use field rod ovens powered by generators
  • Sealing Partial Containers: Use moisture-resistant tape to reseal partial containers
  • Environmental Awareness: Be especially careful in high humidity, rain, or morning dew conditions

Quality Control Measures

Implement these procedures to ensure electrode quality:

  • Receiving Inspection: Check packaging integrity and certification
  • Storage Documentation: Track storage conditions and exposure time
  • Test Welds: Perform test welds before critical applications
  • Coating Inspection: Visually inspect for damage or discoloration
  • Moisture Testing: For critical applications, measure moisture content
  • Traceability: Maintain lot numbers and certification records

Develop a "first in, first out" system for electrode inventory to ensure older stock is used before newer stock, reducing the risk of prolonged storage issues.

Electrode Efficiency and Optimization

Understanding Deposition Rates

Deposition rate is the weight of weld metal deposited per unit of time, typically measured in pounds per hour (lb/hr).

Electrode Type 1/8" Diameter 5/32" Diameter 3/16" Diameter Relative Rating
E6010/E6011 2-3 lb/hr 3-4 lb/hr 4-5 lb/hr Low
E7018 3-4 lb/hr 4-5 lb/hr 5-7 lb/hr Medium
E7024 4-6 lb/hr 6-9 lb/hr 9-12 lb/hr High
E7028 4-6 lb/hr 6-9 lb/hr 9-12 lb/hr High

Factors Affecting Deposition Rate:

  • Electrode Type: Iron powder electrodes have higher deposition rates
  • Electrode Size: Larger diameters deposit more metal
  • Amperage: Higher amperage increases deposition (within limits)
  • Welding Position: Flat position allows highest deposition rates
  • Arc Time: Percentage of time spent actually welding vs. changing electrodes

Operating Factor and Arc Time

Operating factor (also called duty cycle or arc time percentage) is the percentage of working time that the welder is actually depositing weld metal.

  • Typical Stick Welding Operating Factors:
    • Ideal Conditions: 30-40%
    • Field Work: 15-25%
    • Difficult Positions: 10-20%
  • Factors Reducing Operating Factor:
    • Electrode changes
    • Slag removal
    • Repositioning work
    • Inspection time
    • Rest periods

Improving Welding Efficiency

Electrode Selection for Efficiency:

  • Use largest practical electrode diameter
  • Consider high-deposition electrodes for filling operations
  • Use longer electrodes when available (14" vs. 12")
  • Match electrode type to application for fewer defects/repairs

Procedural Improvements:

  • Organize workspace to minimize movement
  • Keep electrodes easily accessible
  • Optimize work positioning for flat welding when possible
  • Use fixtures and positioners to maximize flat/horizontal welding
  • Plan weld sequences to minimize repositioning
  • Use larger electrodes for fill passes

Common Efficiency Mistakes:

  • Using too small an electrode for thick materials
  • Improper amperage settings (too low)
  • Poor fit-up requiring excessive filling
  • Using all-position electrodes for flat-only work
  • Overpreparation of joints (excessive beveling)
  • Poor storage leading to defects and repairs

Cost Comparison of Electrode Types

When calculating true welding costs, consider these factors beyond electrode price:

Cost Factor E6010/E6011 E7018 E7024
Relative Electrode Cost Low Medium Medium-High
Deposition Rate Low Medium High
Deposition Efficiency 60-70% 65-75% 80-90%
Operating Factor Medium Medium High
Position Limitations All Positions All Positions Flat/Horizontal
Storage Requirements Minimal Rod Oven Moderate

Total Weld Cost Formula:

Total Cost = (Electrode Cost/Deposition Efficiency) + Labor Cost + Overhead + Power Cost

Where:

  • Labor Cost = (Metal Deposited ÷ Deposition Rate) × (Labor Rate ÷ Operating Factor)
  • Deposition Efficiency = Weight of metal deposited ÷ Weight of electrodes used
