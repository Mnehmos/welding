Effective Practice Philosophy

Becoming proficient at stick welding requires consistent, focused practice with clear objectives and feedback.

Structured Approach to Skill Development

Like any craft, welding mastery follows a progression from foundational skills to advanced techniques:

Stage 1: Fundamentals

  • Basic machine operation
  • Striking and maintaining an arc
  • Running straight beads
  • Consistent travel speed and arc length

Stage 2: Basic Techniques

  • Controlling the weld pool
  • Simple joint types in flat position
  • Common manipulation patterns
  • Basic visual inspection

Stage 3: Position and Joint Mastery

  • Welding in all positions
  • All five basic joint types
  • Multi-pass techniques
  • Defect identification and correction

Stage 4: Advanced Applications

  • Special techniques and materials
  • Code-quality welds
  • Problem-solving challenging applications
  • Efficiency and consistency optimization

Key Principles for Effective Practice

Deliberate Focus

Practice with specific goals rather than aimless welding. Target particular skills or techniques in each session.

Progressive Challenge

Continually push beyond comfort zone. Once a skill becomes easy, increase difficulty by changing position, joint type, or parameters.

Immediate Feedback

Inspect each weld critically. Break test samples, cut cross-sections, and analyze results to identify areas for improvement.

Regular Repetition

Consistent practice is better than occasional marathon sessions. Aim for regular, focused practice rather than infrequent lengthy sessions.

Reflective Analysis

Keep notes on successes and challenges. Document settings, techniques, and results to identify patterns and improvements.

Varied Materials

Practice on different thicknesses and types of steel. Varying materials builds adaptability and deeper understanding.

Setting Up for Practice

Materials Needed:

  • Metal:
    • Mild steel plates (1/8" to 1/4" thickness)
    • Various sizes for different exercises
    • Scrap pieces for practice beads
  • Electrodes:
    • E6013: Easiest for beginners
    • E7018: For structural practice
    • E6010/E6011: For root pass practice
    • 3/32" and 1/8" diameters most common for practice
  • Tools:
    • Chipping hammer and wire brush
    • Angle grinder with grinding and cutting wheels
    • Metal file for edge preparation
    • C-clamps or welding clamps
    • Welding magnets for positioning
    • Soapstone or marker for layout
    • Steel rule and square
  • Safety Equipment:
    • All standard welding PPE
    • Fire extinguisher nearby
    • Proper ventilation

Workspace Setup:

  • Stable welding table at comfortable height
  • Good lighting positioned to avoid shadows
  • Organized tool storage within reach
  • Adequate space for different positions
  • Fire-resistant surroundings
  • Nearby bench or vise for preparation

Create a practice coupon jig by welding angle iron to a plate. This creates a stable platform that allows you to practice in different positions by simply repositioning the jig without having to reclamp your work.

Beginner Practice Exercises

Arc Starting Practice

Objective:

Develop consistent, reliable arc starts with minimal sticking or excessive striking.

Setup:

  • Flat steel plate (at least 1/4" thick)
  • E6013 electrodes, 1/8" diameter
  • Amperage set to middle of recommended range

Exercise:

  1. Practice scratch start technique:
    • Hold electrode at 20-30Â° angle
    • Scratch lightly like striking a match
    • Once arc initiates, lift to proper arc length
  2. Practice tap start technique:
    • Hold electrode perpendicular to plate
    • Tap electrode lightly on surface
    • Lift slightly as contact is made
  3. Start and maintain arc for 3-5 seconds, then break arc
  4. Move to different spot on plate
  5. Repeat 20-30 times per session

Success Criteria:

  • Consistent starts with minimal attempts
  • Little to no electrode sticking
  • Smooth transition to welding arc length
  • Can start arc in any position reliably

Progression:

  • Start with E6013, then progress to E7018
  • Try different diameter electrodes
  • Practice starting in different positions
  • Try starts at different points in a joint

Running Straight Beads

Objective:

Develop the ability to run straight, consistent stringer beads with proper width, height, and appearance.

Setup:

  • Flat steel plate, 1/8" to 1/4" thick
  • Draw straight lines with soapstone as guides
  • E6013 electrodes, 1/8" diameter
  • Amperage set to middle of recommended range

Exercise:

  1. Position yourself comfortably with clear view of workpiece
  2. Strike arc at beginning of guide line
  3. Hold consistent arc length (approximately electrode diameter)
  4. Move electrode along guide line at steady speed
  5. Focus on three key variables:
    • Arc length: Keep consistent distance
    • Travel speed: Maintain steady pace
    • Travel angle: Hold 10-15Â° drag angle
  6. Complete bead to end of line
  7. After cooling, clean and inspect bead
  8. Run parallel beads with small gap between

Success Criteria:

  • Straight, uniform bead following guide line
  • Consistent width (approximately 2Ã electrode diameter)
  • Even height with slight crown
  • Regular ripple pattern
  • Good tie-in at edges
  • Minimal spatter

Progression:

  • Increase length of beads
  • Remove guide lines once confident
  • Vary travel speed to observe effects
  • Try with different electrodes (E7018)
  • Practice on vertical surface (up and down)

Weave Patterns

Objective:

Master basic electrode manipulation patterns to control weld bead width and penetration.

Setup:

  • Flat steel plate, 1/8" to 1/4" thick
  • Draw parallel lines to indicate desired width
  • E6013 electrodes, 1/8" diameter
  • Amperage set to middle of recommended range

Exercise:

  1. Practice simple side-to-side (zigzag) weave:
    • Strike arc at starting point
    • Move electrode side to side in straight pattern
    • Maintain consistent rhythm
    • Keep weave width to 3Ã electrode diameter maximum
  2. Practice C-shaped weave:
    • Move electrode in curved C pattern
    • Slight pause at edges for good tie-in
    • Maintain forward progress
  3. Practice J-shaped weave:
    • Move electrode in J pattern
    • Emphasize pause at top of J
    • Good for vertical up welding
  4. Clean and inspect each pattern
  5. Compare differences in appearance and characteristics

Success Criteria:

  • Consistent weave pattern throughout bead
  • Even width matching guide lines
  • Good tie-in at edges
  • Uniform ripple pattern
  • Smooth transitions between weaves

Progression:

  • Increase width gradually (within limits)
  • Try triangle/Christmas tree pattern
  • Practice in vertical position
  • Attempt box/square pattern
  • Apply patterns to actual joints

Basic Lap and T-Joints

Objective:

Apply basic skills to simple joint configurations, developing the ability to create proper fillet welds.

Setup:

  • Two steel plates (1/8" to 3/16" thick)
  • Clamps or magnets to hold position
  • E6013 electrodes, 1/8" diameter
  • Amperage set to middle of recommended range

Exercise:

  1. Lap Joint Setup:
    • Place one plate partially over another
    • Ensure good contact between plates
    • Tack weld corners to maintain position
  2. T-Joint Setup:
    • Position one plate perpendicular to another
    • Ensure 90Â° angle and good contact
    • Tack weld ends to maintain position
  3. For each joint type:
    • Position electrode at 45Â° angle to both plates
    • Strike arc and establish weld pool
    • Maintain consistent travel speed
    • Focus on creating properly sized fillet
    • Complete entire joint length
  4. Clean and inspect welds

Success Criteria:

  • Equal leg size (approximately same as material thickness)
  • Straight, consistent bead
  • Slight concave to flat face profile
  • Good fusion to both plates
  • No undercut or overlap
  • Full penetration into joint root

Progression:

  • Weld opposite side of joints
  • Try different size fillets
  • Apply weave patterns for wider coverage
  • Practice in horizontal position
  • Use E7018 electrodes
  • Test weld strength by breaking samples

Intermediate Practice Exercises

Positional Welding Practice

Objective:

Develop the skills to weld effectively in horizontal, vertical, and overhead positions.

Setup:

  • Steel plates (1/8" to 3/16" thick)
  • Positioning jig or positioner
  • E6013 and E7018 electrodes, 1/8" diameter
  • Adjust amperage:
    • Horizontal: 90-100% of flat position amperage
    • Vertical: 70-85% of flat position amperage
    • Overhead: 70-80% of flat position amperage

Horizontal (2G/2F) Practice:

  1. Position plates vertically with joint horizontal
  2. For fillet welds (2F):
    • Position electrode to direct more heat to vertical plate
    • Use slight upward angle to counter gravity
    • Maintain faster travel speed than flat position
    • Keep weld pool small and controlled
  3. For groove welds (2G):
    • Maintain perpendicular position to plates
    • Use whip or pause technique to allow solidification
    • Control keyhole carefully on root passes

Vertical (3G/3F) Practice:

  1. Position plates vertically with weld line vertical
  2. For vertical-up welding:
    • Use weave patterns (J, triangle, or Christmas tree)
    • Pause at edges of weave to ensure tie-in
    • Control puddle carefully, letting it solidify partially before advancing
    • Maintain consistent forward progress
  3. For vertical-down (thin materials only):
    • Use higher travel speed
    • Keep arc on leading edge of puddle
    • Maintain very short arc length

Overhead (4G/4F) Practice:

  1. Position plates overhead (safely secured)
  2. Use proper PPE for spatter protection
  3. Keep weld pool small (max 3Ã electrode diameter)
  4. Use faster travel speed than other positions
  5. Maintain very short arc length
  6. Use stringer beads rather than weaves initially
  7. Position body for stability and comfort

Success Criteria:

  • Complete penetration of joint
  • Proper contour for position (slight convexity acceptable in vertical/overhead)
  • Good fusion at edges
  • No sag, drips, or excessive reinforcement
  • Minimal spatter
  • No undercutting or overlap

Progression:

  • Start with fillet welds, progress to groove welds
  • Increase material thickness
  • Practice all joints (butt, lap, T, corner, edge) in each position
  • Progress from E6013 to E7018 electrodes
  • Increase length and complexity of welds
  • Perform break tests to evaluate penetration and strength

Open Root Butt Joints

Objective:

Master the challenging technique of open root butt welding, a critical skill for pipe and structural welding.

Setup:

  • Two steel plates, 3/16" to 1/4" thick
  • Bevel one edge of each plate (30-35Â° bevel angle)
  • Position with 1/16" to 3/32" root gap
  • E6010 or E6011 electrodes, 1/8" diameter for root
  • E7018 electrodes, 1/8" diameter for fill/cap
  • Set amperage appropriate for electrode and position

Exercise:

  1. Root Pass Technique:
    • Position electrode to direct arc at root opening
    • Establish keyhole (opening through joint)
    • Keyhole should be approximately 2/3 electrode diameter
    • Use slight side-to-side motion to ensure fusion to both bevels
    • Watch keyhole as you progress, maintaining consistent size
    • Control travel speed to maintain keyhole without burn-through
  2. Hot Pass:
    • Clean root pass thoroughly
    • Apply second pass (hot pass) with E7018
    • Focus on good tie-in to root pass
    • Keep hot pass narrow with minimal reinforcement
  3. Fill and Cap Passes:
    • Add fill passes as needed to build up joint
    • Finish with cap pass, focusing on appearance
    • Ensure good tie-in to base metal
    • Create slight crown on cap pass

Success Criteria:

  • Root Pass:
    • Complete penetration (visible from back side)
    • Consistent back bead (1/16" to 3/32" wide)
    • No burn-through or excessive drop-through
    • Good fusion to both bevels
  • Completed Joint:
    • Full fusion throughout
    • No internal defects (verify with bend test)
    • Uniform cap appearance
    • No undercut or overlap

Progression:

  • Start in flat position, progress to other positions
  • Vary root gap and land dimensions
  • Practice with different bevel angles
  • Try thicker materials
  • Perform guided bend tests to evaluate root quality
  • Practice root passes on actual pipe joints

A key skill for open root welding is maintaining the keyhole. Practice watching the "window" to the other side, which indicates full penetration, and adjusting your travel speed and angle to keep it consistent.

Multi-Pass Welding

Objective:

Develop the ability to build up weld joints on thicker materials using multiple passes with proper bead placement and sequencing.

Setup:

  • Steel plates, 3/8" to 1/2" thick
  • Prepared V-groove or T-joint
  • E6010/E6011 for root (if open root)
  • E7018 for fill and cap passes
  • 1/8" electrodes for most passes
  • Chipping hammer and wire brush for cleaning

Exercise:

  1. Root Pass:
    • Apply root pass focusing on complete penetration
    • If open root, use keyhole technique
    • If backing, ensure complete fusion to backing
  2. Hot Pass:
    • Clean root pass thoroughly
    • Apply hot pass while root is still warm
    • Focus on cleaning up root pass and reinforcing
  3. Fill Passes:
    • Plan bead placement sequence
    • For wide joints, use multiple side-by-side stringers
    • For deep joints, layer multiple passes
    • Clean thoroughly between each pass
    • Ensure good tie-in to previous passes and sidewalls
  4. Cap Pass:
    • Apply final layer with focus on appearance
    • Create slightly convex profile
    • Ensure good tie-in to base metal edges
    • Achieve uniform ripple pattern

Success Criteria:

  • Complete fusion between all passes
  • No slag inclusions or internal defects
  • Good fusion to sidewalls throughout
  • Proper reinforcement (not excessive)
  • No undercut at edges
  • Uniform appearance of cap pass

Progression:

  • Increase joint thickness and width
  • Practice in different positions
  • Try different pass sequences and patterns
  • Cut completed joints to inspect cross-section
  • Perform bend tests to evaluate joint quality
  • Try more challenging joint configurations

Advanced Practice Exercises

Creating Test Coupons

Objective:

Practice creating code-quality test coupons suitable for certification testing, developing precision and consistency to meet specific standards.

Setup:

  • Steel plates to code specifications (typically 3/8" thick, 6" Ã 8")
  • Proper joint preparation to code requirements
  • Specified electrodes by code (typically E6010 root, E7018 fill/cap)
  • Positioning fixtures for specific test position
  • Temperature measuring tools if preheat or interpass control required

Exercise:

  1. Preparation Phase:
    • Precisely measure and cut plates to specification
    • Prepare groove to exact dimensions (bevel angle, root face, root opening)
    • Clean thoroughly and remove all contaminants
    • Position and tack per code requirements
  2. Practice following code procedures:
    • Preheat if required (typically for thicker materials)
    • Document parameters (amperage, voltage, travel speed)
    • Follow specified electrode requirements
    • Maintain specified interpass temperature
    • Use only allowed positions and techniques
  3. Test coupon types to practice:
    • Groove weld in plate (1G, 2G, 3G, 4G positions)
    • Fillet weld in plate (1F, 2F, 3F, 4F positions)
    • Groove weld in pipe (1G, 2G, 5G, 6G positions)
    • Multiple qualification coupons in one setup (if permitted)

Success Criteria:

  • Visual Inspection:
    • Meets all visual acceptance criteria in applicable code
    • No excessive reinforcement or undercut
    • Proper bead profile and appearance
    • No surface porosity or cracks
  • Destructive Testing:
    • Cut test specimens per code requirements
    • Perform guided bend tests (root and face bends)
    • Evaluate according to acceptance criteria
  • Repeatability:
    • Consistent results across multiple test coupons
    • Ability to produce passing welds consistently

Progression:

  • Start with simpler positions (1G/1F)
  • Progress to more challenging positions (3G, 4G, 6G)
  • Reduce preparation time while maintaining quality
  • Practice under timed conditions (simulating test environment)
  • Implement full documentation as required in actual testing

When preparing for certification tests, practice exactly as you'll perform during the test. Use the same body position, tools, and techniques. Muscle memory is crucial for consistent performance under the pressure of testing.

Pipe Welding Practice

Objective:

Develop the specialized skills required for pipe welding, including all-position techniques and working with curved surfaces.

Setup:

  • Steel pipe sections (4"-6" diameter, schedule 40 typical for practice)
  • Pipe beveling tool or grinder for joint preparation
  • Pipe stand or positioner
  • E6010/E6011 for root pass (3/32" or 1/8")
  • E7018 for fill and cap (1/8")
  • Pipe clamps or fixtures for alignment

Exercise:

  1. Pipe Joint Preparation:
    • Prepare 37.5Â° bevel (75Â° included angle)
    • Establish 1/16" root face
    • Align with 3/32" root gap
    • Tack weld in at least three places
  2. 1G (Rolled) Position Practice:
    • Position pipe horizontally and rotate during welding
    • All welding done in flat position
    • Easiest position for beginners
    • Focus on maintaining consistent root pass around pipe
  3. 2G (Horizontal) Position Practice:
    • Position pipe vertically with weld seam horizontal
    • Similar to plate 2G position
    • Practice maintaining consistent travel speed around curve
  4. 5G (Horizontal Fixed) Position Practice:
    • Position pipe horizontally without rotation
    • Requires welding in flat, vertical, and overhead positions
    • Typically start at bottom (6 o'clock) and work up both sides
    • Adjust technique for each position around pipe
  5. 6G (45Â° Fixed) Position Practice:
    • Position pipe at 45Â° angle without rotation
    • Most challenging position (covers all positions)
    • Requires high level of skill and adaptability
    • Focus on consistent penetration around entire joint

Success Criteria:

  • Root Pass:
    • Complete penetration around entire joint
    • Consistent root bead visible inside pipe
    • No burn-through or excessive penetration
    • Good fusion to both pipe walls
  • Completed Joint:
    • Uniform bead appearance around entire circumference
    • Proper reinforcement without excess
    • No undercut or overlap
    • Good tie-in at all stop/start points
  • Test Results:
    • Passes visual inspection
    • Cross-sections show complete fusion
    • Bend tests show no defects

Progression:

  • Start with 1G position, progress to more difficult positions
  • Increase pipe diameter and wall thickness
  • Reduce root gap to increase difficulty
  • Practice different pipe sizes and schedules
  • Try pipe-to-plate and pipe-to-fitting joints
  • Simulate field conditions with limited access

Thin Material Welding

Objective:

Master the precise control required to weld thin materials (16 gauge to 1/8") without burn-through or distortion.

Setup:

  • Thin steel sheets (16, 18, or 20 gauge)
  • Small diameter electrodes (3/32" or 5/64")
  • E6013 electrodes (easiest for thin materials)
  • Lower amperage settings (typically 40-70 amps)
  • Copper backing bar (optional, helps dissipate heat)
  • Clamping fixtures to control distortion

Exercise:

  1. Preparation:
    • Clean material thoroughly
    • Fit with minimal gap
    • Use proper clamping to dissipate heat
    • Tack weld at small intervals
  2. Technique Practice:
    • Use stringer beads (no weaving)
    • Maintain faster travel speed than normal
    • Try whip or step technique to control heat input
    • Practice skip welding to distribute heat
    • Use short arc length
  3. Joint Types:
    • Practice lap joints (easiest for thin material)
    • Progress to butt joints with square edges
    • Try edge joints for sheet metal applications
    • Practice T-joints with minimal heat input

Success Criteria:

  • No burn-through or holes
  • Complete fusion at joint
  • Minimal distortion of base material
  • Appropriate sized weld for material thickness
  • Smooth appearance with minimal spatter
  • No undercut or overlap

Progression:

  • Start with thicker material (16 gauge), progress to thinner
  • Reduce amperage as skill increases
  • Remove backing as control improves
  • Practice increasingly longer welds
  • Try welding thin materials in vertical and overhead positions
  • Create practical thin-material projects (sheet metal boxes, etc.)

For welding thin materials, think of "painting" the weld rather than "digging" into the metal. A light touch and quick hand are essential. Better to have too little heat and make multiple passes than too much heat and burn through.

Weld Evaluation Methods

Effective practice requires objective evaluation of weld quality. These methods help identify strengths and weaknesses in your welds.

Visual Inspection Techniques

Surface Appearance Assessment

  • Bead Geometry:
    • Width: Should be consistent along length
    • Height: Slight crown (1/16") without excess reinforcement
    • Contour: Smooth transition to base metal
  • Ripple Pattern:
    • Uniform spacing and size
    • Even overlapping pattern
    • Consistent along entire length
  • Edge Fusion:
    • No undercut (groove at weld toe)
    • No overlap (weld metal folded over without fusion)
    • Smooth transition from weld to base metal
  • Surface Discontinuities:
    • No visible porosity (holes)
    • No surface cracks
    • No slag inclusions visible on surface
    • Minimal spatter

Use good lighting and examine from multiple angles. A small pocket mirror can help see hard-to-reach areas, and a magnifying glass helps spot small defects.

Destructive Testing

Break Tests

Break tests are simple yet effective ways to evaluate weld strength and penetration:

  • Fillet Weld Break Test:
    • For lap or T-joints
    • Place joint in vise with weld up
    • Strike unwelded portion with hammer until break occurs
    • Examine fracture surface:
      • Good weld: Breaks through base metal or shows full fusion
      • Poor weld: Breaks along fusion line or shows lack of penetration
  • Nick-Break Test:
    • For butt joints
    • Cut sample across weld (typically 1" wide)
    • Nick sides at weld centerline
    • Break by bending or striking
    • Examine fracture surface for:
      • Porosity
      • Slag inclusions
      • Incomplete fusion
      • Cracks

Guided Bend Tests

More formal test for evaluating ductility and fusion:

  • Equipment Needed:
    • Bend fixture (can be improvised with vise and mandrel)
    • Test specimens cut to standard size (typically 1.5" Ã 6")
  • Types of Bend Tests:
    • Root bend: Tests root pass fusion
    • Face bend: Tests cap pass fusion
    • Side bend: Tests overall joint fusion (for thick material)
  • Procedure:
    • Cut and prepare sample to specified dimensions
    • Remove excess reinforcement by grinding flush
    • Bend specimen around mandrel to 180Â°
    • Examine outer curved surface for defects
  • Acceptance Criteria:
    • No open discontinuities exceeding 1/8" in any direction
    • Total sum of defects not exceeding 3/8"
    • No corner cracks (unless proven not from weld)

Macro-Etching and Cross-Sectioning

Cross-Section Examination

Reveals internal weld structure and defects:

  • Equipment Needed:
    • Metal cutting equipment (saw, grinder)
    • Polishing equipment (files, sandpaper, polishing compound)
    • Etching solution (5% nital for steel)
    • Magnifying glass or microscope
  • Procedure:
    • Cut section perpendicular to weld direction
    • File and sand cut surface (progressing to fine grit)
    • Polish to mirror finish
    • Apply etching solution briefly
    • Rinse with water and dry
    • Examine under good lighting with magnification
  • What to Look For:
    • Penetration depth and profile
    • Fusion between passes
    • Internal defects (porosity, inclusions)
    • Heat-affected zone characteristics
    • Weld reinforcement profile

Take photos of your cross-sections for future reference. Comparing sections over time helps track your progress and identify consistent issues that need attention.

Tracking Progress and Skill Development

Maintaining a Welding Practice Log

A welding log helps track your progress, identify patterns, and provide structured feedback for improvement.

Essential Log Information:

  • Date and Duration: When and how long you practiced
  • Exercise Description: Specific skill or joint practiced
  • Materials Used: Type, thickness, preparation
  • Welding Parameters:
    • Electrode type and size
    • Amperage and polarity
    • Position
    • Technique used
  • Results and Observations:
    • Visual assessment
    • Test results (if performed)
    • Specific challenges encountered
    • What worked well
  • Areas for Improvement: Specific skills to focus on next
  • Photos or Sketches: Visual documentation of welds

Log Format Options:

  • Digital Spreadsheet: Easy to organize and search
  • Notebook or Journal: Simple and accessible during practice
  • Photo Journal: Emphasizes visual documentation
  • Mobile App: Convenient for shop environment

Consistency is more important than format. Choose a logging method you'll actually use regularly. Even brief notes after each session are better than detailed logs that are rarely completed.

Finding Feedback and Guidance

External feedback accelerates skill development by identifying issues you might miss and providing new perspectives.

Sources of Feedback:

  • Formal Instruction:
    • Community college welding courses
    • Technical school programs
    • Manufacturing extension programs
    • Union apprenticeship programs
  • Experienced Welders:
    • Connect with professionals in the field
    • Bring samples of your work for evaluation
    • Ask specific questions about technique
    • Request demonstration of challenging skills
  • Online Communities:
    • Welding forums and discussion boards
    • Social media groups dedicated to welding
    • Video sharing platforms for technique demonstration
    • Post photos of your work for critique
  • Certification Testing:
    • Even if you don't pass, provides professional evaluation
    • Objective assessment of your skills
    • Identifies specific areas needing improvement
  • Books and Resources:
    • Welding textbooks with evaluation criteria
    • AWS standards for visual inspection
    • Technical manuals with defect identification guides

Making the Most of Feedback:

  • Ask specific questions rather than general ones
  • Listen without defensiveness
  • Take notes on suggestions
  • Implement one change at a time
  • Follow up to show your progress
  • Seek multiple perspectives on challenging issues

When seeking feedback, bring both your best work and pieces you're struggling with. Seeing both helps instructors or experienced welders identify patterns in your technique that might be affecting both.

Practice Resources and Materials

Finding Practice Materials

Acquiring affordable materials for practice is essential for regular skill development.

Budget-Friendly Material Sources:

  • Metal Scrapyards:
    • Sell by weight at fraction of retail cost
    • Wide variety of sizes and shapes
    • Call ahead to ask about minimum purchases
    • Bring appropriate vehicle and safety gear
  • Metal Fabrication Shops:
    • Often have drop or cutoffs available
    • Some shops sell scrap by the pound
    • Build relationship with local shops
    • Cleaner and more consistent than scrapyards
  • Construction Sites:
    • Ask permission from site supervisor
    • Look for structural steel cutoffs
    • Offer to help clean up in exchange for scrap
  • Online Classifieds:
    • Search for "metal scrap" or "steel plate"
    • Check industrial equipment listings
    • Look for business closures or equipment upgrades
  • Metal Suppliers:
    • Ask about remnant or cutoff bins
    • Often sell odd sizes at discount
    • Bulk purchases of common practice sizes

Preparation Tips:

  • Clean all scrap material before welding
  • Remove paint, oil, and rust with grinder
  • Cut to usable practice sizes
  • Organize by thickness and type
  • Store properly to prevent rust
