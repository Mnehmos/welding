Introduction to Welding Different Metals

While stick welding (SMAW) is often associated with carbon steel, this versatile process can be used to join a variety of metals with the proper electrodes, techniques, and preparation. Understanding how different materials respond to the welding process is essential for producing high-quality welds.

Key Material Considerations

When welding any material, several key properties affect the welding process:

  • Thermal Conductivity: How quickly heat travels through the material
  • Thermal Expansion: How much the material expands when heated
  • Melting Temperature: The point at which the metal transitions from solid to liquid
  • Oxidation Tendency: How readily the material reacts with oxygen when heated
  • Alloy Elements: Additional metals that modify the base material's properties
  • Microstructure: The internal grain structure of the metal
Material Thermal Conductivity Melting Range Weldability with SMAW Common Challenges
Mild Steel Medium 2600-2800°F Excellent Few challenges
Stainless Steel Low 2550-2750°F Good Distortion, carbide precipitation
Cast Iron Low 2100-2200°F Fair Cracking, hardening
Aluminum Very High 1200-1300°F Poor to Fair Oxide layer, rapid heat dissipation
High-Carbon Steel Medium 2600-2800°F Fair Hardening, cracking

Carbon Steel

Carbon steel is the most common material welded with the SMAW process. It's categorized by its carbon content, which affects its mechanical properties and weldability.

Carbon Steel Grades

Low Carbon Steel (Mild Steel)

  • Carbon Content: 0.05% to 0.30%
  • Examples: A36, 1018, 1020
  • Properties: Good ductility, formability, weldability
  • Applications: Structural shapes, plate, pipe, general fabrication
  • Weldability: Excellent with minimal special procedures

Medium Carbon Steel

  • Carbon Content: 0.30% to 0.60%
  • Examples: 1040, 1045, 4140
  • Properties: Increased strength and hardness, reduced ductility
  • Applications: Machinery components, shafts, gears
  • Weldability: Good with proper procedures

High Carbon Steel

  • Carbon Content: 0.60% to 1.00%
  • Examples: 1060, 1095, tool steels
  • Properties: High strength and hardness, low ductility
  • Applications: Cutting tools, springs, wear-resistant parts
  • Weldability: Fair to poor, requires special procedures

Carbon Steel Preparation

  1. Cleaning: Remove oil, grease, paint, rust, and mill scale from the weld area
  2. Joint Preparation:
    • Under 1/8" thickness: Square butt joint
    • 1/8" to 3/8" thickness: Single V-groove (60° included angle)
    • Over 3/8" thickness: Double V-groove for heavy sections
  3. Fit-Up: Root gap of 1/16" to 1/8" for full penetration joints
  4. Tack Welds: Place tack welds to maintain alignment

Carbon Steel Electrodes

Steel Type Recommended Electrode Alternative Notes
Mild Steel E6013, E7018 E6010, E6011 E6013 for general purpose, E7018 for structural
Medium Carbon E7018 E8018-B2 Low hydrogen to prevent cracking
High Carbon E7018-1, E8018-B2 E9018-B3 Preheat and post-weld heat treatment important

Carbon Steel Welding Techniques

Mild Steel:

  • Standard drag angle (5-15°)
  • Stringer or weave beads as needed
  • No preheat typically required for sections under 1"
  • Interpass temperature not critical (under 500°F recommended)

Medium Carbon Steel:

  • Preheat to 300-500°F for sections over 1/2" thick
  • Control heat input (not too hot, not too cold)
  • Use stringer beads for better mechanical properties
  • Consider tempering passes for final layers
  • Slow cooling after welding (cover with insulating blanket)

High Carbon Steel:

  • Preheat to 500-700°F
  • Maintain interpass temperature
  • Use low hydrogen electrodes
  • Small stringer beads to control heat input
  • Post-weld heat treatment (tempering at 1100-1300°F)
  • Very slow cooling rate

As carbon content increases, so does the risk of hydrogen cracking. For medium and high carbon steels, proper preheat, low hydrogen electrodes, and controlled cooling are essential.

Stainless Steel

Stainless steel contains a minimum of 10.5% chromium, which forms a passive chromium oxide surface layer that provides corrosion resistance. It's categorized into several types based on microstructure.

Stainless Steel Types

Austenitic Stainless Steel

  • Series: 300 series (304, 316, 321, etc.)
  • Characteristics: Non-magnetic, excellent corrosion resistance
  • Alloying Elements: 16-26% Chromium, 6-22% Nickel
  • Applications: Food processing, chemical equipment, architectural
  • Weldability: Good, but susceptible to sensitization

Ferritic Stainless Steel

  • Series: 400 series (430, 409, etc.)
  • Characteristics: Magnetic, moderate corrosion resistance
  • Alloying Elements: 10.5-27% Chromium, minimal Nickel
  • Applications: Automotive exhaust, indoor architectural
  • Weldability: Fair, tends to grain growth

Martensitic Stainless Steel

  • Series: 400 series (410, 420, 440)
  • Characteristics: Magnetic, hardenable, moderate corrosion resistance
  • Alloying Elements: 11.5-18% Chromium, 0.1-1.2% Carbon
  • Applications: Cutlery, surgical instruments, valve components
  • Weldability: Poor to fair, requires special procedures

Duplex Stainless Steel

  • Examples: 2205, 2507
  • Characteristics: Higher strength, good corrosion resistance
  • Microstructure: Mixed austenitic and ferritic
  • Applications: Chemical processing, off-shore, pulp and paper
  • Weldability: Good with proper procedures

Stainless Steel Welding Challenges

Sensitization:

When stainless steel is heated to 800-1500°F, chromium can combine with carbon to form chromium carbides at grain boundaries, depleting the adjacent areas of chromium and reducing corrosion resistance. This is known as sensitization.

Distortion:

Stainless steel has a higher coefficient of thermal expansion and lower thermal conductivity than carbon steel, making it more prone to warping and distortion.

Hot Cracking:

Austenitic stainless steels can be susceptible to hot cracking, especially with high restraint or improper filler metal selection.

High-Temperature Scaling:

Stainless steel can form colored oxide layers when heated, which may need to be removed in certain applications.

Stainless Steel Preparation

  1. Cleaning: Use stainless-specific tools to avoid cross-contamination
    • Remove all oil, grease, paint, and dirt
    • Clean with acetone or alcohol (avoid chlorinated solvents)
    • Use stainless steel wire brushes only (no carbon steel tools)
  2. Joint Design: Similar to carbon steel but with wider root gaps (3/32" typical)
  3. Tack Welds: Use more tack welds to control distortion
  4. Fixturing: Use strong clamping to control movement during welding

Stainless Steel Electrodes

Base Metal Recommended Electrode Characteristics
304/304L E308/E308L Matching composition for general corrosion resistance
316/316L E316/E316L Added molybdenum for improved corrosion resistance
321/347 E347 Stabilized grades for high-temperature service
410/420 E410/E410NiMo Martensitic grades, may require preheat
430 E430 Ferritic stainless, limited applications
Dissimilar (304 to Carbon Steel) E309/E309L Higher chromium and nickel for dilution accommodation

The "L" designation in stainless electrodes (E308L, E316L) indicates low carbon content (0.03% max), which reduces the risk of sensitization and improves resistance to intergranular corrosion.

Stainless Steel Welding Techniques

Heat Input and Travel Speed:

  • Use lower heat input than carbon steel (15-20% lower amperage)
  • Faster travel speed to reduce heat-affected zone
  • Use stringer beads rather than weaves when possible
  • Maintain shorter arc length

Distortion Control:

  • Use strong fixturing and clamping
  • Apply skip or backstep welding techniques
  • Balance welds on opposite sides of the workpiece
  • Minimize heat input

Post-Weld Treatment:

  • Remove heat tint (discoloration) with stainless steel pickling paste
  • Grind and polish welds to restore corrosion resistance
  • Consider post-weld heat treatment for sensitive applications
  • Passivate surface to restore protective oxide layer

When welding austenitic stainless steel with SMAW, use DC+ polarity for most electrodes. This provides better arc stability and penetration control for these materials.

Cast Iron

Cast iron is an iron alloy with 2.0-4.0% carbon and 1-3% silicon. The high carbon content makes it challenging to weld, as it forms brittle structures that are prone to cracking. However, with proper techniques, cast iron can be successfully welded with the SMAW process.

Cast Iron Types

Gray Cast Iron

  • Characteristics: Carbon in flake form, gray fracture surface
  • Properties: Good compressive strength, dampens vibration
  • Applications: Engine blocks, machine bases, pipe
  • Weldability: Fair with proper procedures

Ductile (Nodular) Cast Iron

  • Characteristics: Carbon in spherical form
  • Properties: Higher tensile strength and ductility than gray iron
  • Applications: Crankshafts, heavy equipment components
  • Weldability: Better than gray iron

White Cast Iron

  • Characteristics: Carbon as iron carbide, white fracture surface
  • Properties: Very hard and brittle
  • Applications: Wear plates, crushers
  • Weldability: Poor, generally considered unweldable

Malleable Cast Iron

  • Characteristics: Heat-treated white cast iron
  • Properties: More ductile than gray iron
  • Applications: Small, complex parts
  • Weldability: Fair with proper procedures

Cast Iron Welding Challenges

High Carbon Content:

The high carbon in cast iron can form hard, brittle structures (martensite) in the heat-affected zone, leading to cracking.

Thermal Stress:

Cast iron has low ductility and cannot accommodate the stresses of welding expansion and contraction, leading to cracking.

Graphite Flakes:

In gray cast iron, graphite flakes create discontinuities that can weaken the weld.

Impurity Content:

Cast iron often contains sulfur, phosphorus, and other elements that can affect weldability.

Cast Iron Preparation

  1. Cleaning: Remove all oil, grease, and dirt
  2. Crack Preparation: For crack repairs
    • Drill stop holes at crack ends to prevent propagation
    • Grind a U or V groove along the crack
    • For thicker sections, use a double-V groove
  3. Preheating:
    • Small, simple parts: 500-800°F
    • Large, complex parts: 800-1200°F
    • Heat slowly and uniformly in a furnace when possible

Cast Iron Welding Electrodes

Electrode Type Advantages Disadvantages Best Applications
ENi-CI (pure nickel)
  • Excellent machinability
  • Minimal base metal dilution
  • Ductile deposit
  • Most expensive option
  • Lower strength than NiFe
  • Color mismatch (silver)
  • Engine blocks
  • Machined surfaces
  • Critical components
ENiFe-CI (nickel-iron)
  • Good machinability
  • Higher strength than pure Ni
  • Economical compared to pure Ni
  • Less machinable than pure Ni
  • Slight color mismatch
  • General purpose repairs
  • Moderate machining needs
  • Higher strength applications
ESt (steel electrodes: E7018, E6013)
  • Lowest cost option
  • Readily available
  • Good color match
  • Hard, difficult to machine
  • High risk of cracking
  • Requires extensive preheating
  • Non-machined surfaces
  • Low-stress applications
  • Cosmetic repairs

Cast Iron Welding Techniques

Preheating:

  • Preheat entire casting when possible
  • Use temperature-indicating crayons to verify preheat
  • Maintain preheat throughout the welding process

Welding Process:

  • Use lowest practical amperage (30-40% lower than for steel)
  • Short arc length
  • Small, stringer beads (no weaving)
  • Short bead segments (2-3" long)
  • Allow cooling between beads
  • Peen each bead while still hot to relieve stress

Post-Weld Cooling:

  • Cool very slowly (50-100°F per hour)
  • Cover with insulating blanket or sand
  • For critical components, return to furnace for controlled cooling
  • Do not quench or rapidly cool under any circumstances

Never quench or rapidly cool cast iron welds. The extreme thermal stress and hardening will almost certainly cause cracking.

Braze Welding Cast Iron

An alternative to fusion welding cast iron is braze welding, which operates at lower temperatures.

  • Process: Uses brass or bronze filler metals (ECuSn-A, ECuSn-C)
  • Temperature: 1600-1800°F (below cast iron melting point)
  • Advantages:
    • Lower temperature means less thermal stress
    • Reduced risk of cracking
    • No hardening of the heat-affected zone
    • Machinable deposit
  • Disadvantages:
    • Lower strength than fusion welding
    • Color mismatch (yellow/bronze)
    • Not suitable for high-temperature applications

Aluminum

Aluminum welding with the SMAW process is challenging due to aluminum's properties. While TIG and MIG are generally preferred for aluminum, stick welding can be used when other processes aren't available.

Aluminum Properties Affecting Welding

  • High Thermal Conductivity: Heat spreads quickly, making it difficult to localize heating
  • Low Melting Point: Melts at around 1200°F (compared to 2700°F for steel)
  • No Color Change: Aluminum doesn't change color when heated, making it hard to judge temperature
  • Oxide Layer: Aluminum oxide melts at 3700°F, much higher than aluminum itself
  • Thermal Expansion: Aluminum expands twice as much as steel when heated
  • Hydrogen Sensitivity: Readily absorbs hydrogen, leading to porosity

Common Aluminum Alloys

1xxx Series (Pure Aluminum)

  • Examples: 1100, 1350
  • Characteristics: Soft, ductile, excellent corrosion resistance
  • Applications: Chemical equipment, electrical conductors
  • Weldability: Good, but low strength welds

3xxx Series (Aluminum-Manganese)

  • Examples: 3003, 3004
  • Characteristics: Moderate strength, non-heat-treatable
  • Applications: Food and beverage containers, roofing
  • Weldability: Good

5xxx Series (Aluminum-Magnesium)

  • Examples: 5052, 5083, 5086
  • Characteristics: Good strength, excellent corrosion resistance
  • Applications: Marine, transportation, pressure vessels
  • Weldability: Excellent

6xxx Series (Aluminum-Magnesium-Silicon)

  • Examples: 6061, 6063
  • Characteristics: Medium strength, heat-treatable
  • Applications: Structural, extrusions, architectural
  • Weldability: Good, but strength reduction in HAZ

Aluminum Preparation

  1. Cleaning: Critical for successful aluminum welding
    • Remove all oil and grease with solvent (acetone or alcohol)
    • Remove oxide layer mechanically (stainless steel brush or scraper)
    • Clean immediately before welding (oxide reforms quickly)
    • Avoid chlorinated solvents which can cause porosity
  2. Joint Design:
    • Wider root gaps than steel (1/8" typical)
    • Wider groove angles (70-80° included angle)
    • Thicker backing bars to handle heat dissipation
  3. Preheating:
    • For thicker sections, preheat to 300-400°F
    • Use temperature-indicating crayons to verify preheat

Aluminum Electrodes

Aluminum stick electrodes have a heavy flux coating designed to remove the oxide layer and shield the molten pool.

Electrode Type Applications Base Materials
E4043 (AlSi5) General purpose, good fluidity 1xxx, 3xxx, 6xxx series
E5356 (AlMg5) Higher strength, marine applications 5xxx series, dissimilar alloys

Aluminum stick electrodes have a short shelf life. Store in a dry, sealed container. Use fresh electrodes for best results.

Aluminum Stick Welding Techniques

Machine Settings:

  • Use DC+ (electrode positive) polarity
  • Higher open circuit voltage (70-100V) for better arc starts
  • Use lower amperage than steel (30-40% less)

Arc Starting:

  • Scratch start can be difficult due to the oxide layer
  • Strike arc on a clean scrap piece of aluminum, then move to the joint
  • Alternatively, scratch start with a sawing motion

Welding Technique:

  • Use a slight push angle (opposite of steel)
  • Short arc length
  • Faster travel speed than steel
  • Straight ahead motion (minimal or no weaving)
  • Work from the clean area into the already welded area when stopping/starting

Aluminum stick welding is challenging. Practice on scrap material first. If possible, use TIG or MIG processes for aluminum welding.

Welding Dissimilar Metals

Welding different metals together presents unique challenges due to differences in melting points, thermal expansion rates, and the potential formation of brittle intermetallic compounds.

Common Dissimilar Metal Combinations

Metal Combination Challenges Recommended Electrode Notes
Carbon Steel to Stainless Steel Carbon migration, different thermal expansion E309/E309L Higher chromium and nickel to handle dilution
Carbon Steel to Cast Iron Carbon migration, hardening, cracking ENiFe-CI Nickel-iron electrode to accommodate both metals
Stainless Steel to Cast Iron Wide property differences, carbon migration ENi-CI Pure nickel provides best compatibility
Low Alloy Steel to Carbon Steel Strength mismatch, potential hydrogen cracking E8018-B2, E7018 Match to higher-strength material, low hydrogen
Aluminum to Steel Not directly fusion weldable N/A Requires specialty processes (friction, explosion, etc.)

Key Considerations for Dissimilar Metal Welding

Dilution Control:

Dilution is the mixing of the base metals with the filler metal. Managing the amount of dilution is critical in dissimilar metal welding:

  • Use lower heat input to minimize dilution
  • Control bead placement to limit base metal melting
  • First layer may require buffer or butter pass
  • Subsequent layers have reduced dilution

Thermal Expansion Differences:

Different metals expand and contract at different rates when heated and cooled:

  • Use strong clamping and fixturing
  • Consider stress-relief techniques
  • Use stringer beads rather than weaves
  • Apply skip welding or backstep techniques

Mechanical Property Transitions:

The weld should provide a suitable transition between dissimilar base metals:

  • Select filler metal with intermediate or higher properties
  • Consider over-matching strength for critical applications
  • Consider ductility requirements to accommodate stress

Buffer Layer Technique

A buffer layer (sometimes called a "butter pass") is a layer of weld metal applied to one of the base metals before joining them together:

  1. Apply buffer layer to the more challenging material
  2. Use a filler metal compatible with both base metals
  3. Clean thoroughly
  4. Join the buffered surface to the other metal

Example: Carbon Steel to Stainless Steel:

  1. Apply E309L buffer to carbon steel
  2. Clean thoroughly
  3. Join buffered carbon steel to stainless using E309L

Example: Carbon Steel to Cast Iron:

  1. Apply ENiFe-CI buffer to cast iron
  2. Clean thoroughly
  3. Join buffered cast iron to carbon steel using ENiFe-CI

When welding dissimilar metals, it's generally best to use a filler metal that matches or over-matches the lower-alloy material in terms of strength, while being compatible with the higher-alloy material in terms of chemistry.

Hardfacing Applications

Hardfacing is the application of hard, wear-resistant materials to surfaces by welding. It's used to extend component life and can be effectively performed with the SMAW process.

Types of Wear and Hardfacing Materials

Wear Type Characteristics Examples Recommended Hardfacing
Abrasion Grinding, scouring, erosive wear Digging equipment, crushers, screens Chromium carbide, complex carbides
Impact Striking, hammering forces Hammers, crushers, rail components Manganese steels, austenitic deposits
Metal-to-Metal Sliding, rolling contact Rollers, shafts, gear teeth Martensitic deposits, tool steels
Combination Multiple wear mechanisms Cutting edges, agricultural tools Layered or composite hardfacing
Corrosion + Wear Chemical attack with wear Chemical processing equipment Stainless steel, cobalt-based alloys

Common Hardfacing Electrodes

Buildup Electrodes

  • Purpose: Restore dimensions before hardfacing
  • Examples: E7018, E8018-B2
  • Hardness: 180-250 HB
  • Applications: Base layer for severely worn components

Martensitic Electrodes

  • Purpose: General-purpose wear resistance
  • Examples: E FeMn-A, E10-UM-250
  • Hardness: 250-450 HB
  • Applications: Metal-to-metal wear, mild abrasion

Chromium Carbide Electrodes

  • Purpose: Severe abrasion resistance
  • Examples: E Fe-Cr-A, E10-UM-60
  • Hardness: 50-65 HRC
  • Applications: Severe abrasion in mining, cement

Manganese Steel Electrodes

  • Purpose: Impact resistance
  • Examples: E FeMn-B, E7-UM-200
  • Hardness: 180-220 HB as-deposited, work hardens to 450+ HB
  • Applications: Crushers, rail components, high-impact areas

Hardfacing Techniques

Preparation:

  • Clean the surface thoroughly
  • Remove all cracks or defects
  • For carbon steel, preheat to 300-500°F for thick sections
  • For manganese steel, avoid preheating (maintain below 500°F)

Application Patterns:

  • Continuous Coverage: Complete surface coverage for severe abrasion
  • Checker Board: Alternating squares for combined impact and abrasion
  • Waffle: Grid pattern for good impact resistance
  • Parallel Beads: Deposits perpendicular to material flow
  • Diamond: Diamond pattern for directional abrasion resistance

Deposition Techniques:

  • Use stringer beads rather than weaves for most applications
  • Limit to 2-3 layers maximum (additional layers increase spalling risk)
  • Maintain interpass temperature per electrode specification
  • Use skip or step patterns to control heat input and distortion
  • Allow natural cooling (no quenching)

Edge Buildup and Maintenance

For cutting edges and wear surfaces, specific techniques help maximize service life:

Edge Preparation:

  1. Grind a 45° bevel on the edge
  2. Apply buildup material if needed
  3. Apply hard-facing material at an angle to the edge
  4. Allow natural cooling
  5. Finish grind as needed

Maintenance Rebuilding:

  • Rebuild before excessive wear occurs
  • Remove old hardfacing if cracked or spalled
  • Apply new hardfacing in the same pattern as original
  • Document wear patterns to optimize future applications

Many hardfacing deposits cannot be flame cut. Mark components clearly to indicate the presence of hardfacing materials and specify appropriate cutting methods (abrasive cutting, machining, etc.).
